## Travel Charging Repetition

At some point in the process of packing for every trip I take (especially now with small children) I make a point to "figur eout which chargers we need and not take anything extra" and think to myself "I should just have this done from last time, I should figure this out once and for all" and... I never do that.

Packing travel chargers is not a hard task (and getting easier over time, thank you USB-C) but its _annoying_ to spend any time at all thinking this through.

Keep things simple, here is what I pack for myself on say a work trip:

- MacBook (or work stuff, and I like "the big screen for important stuff", I'm a millenial after all)
- Airpods Max (Old Lightning port version)
- Airpods Pro (yea thats right, two different AirPods)
- Apple Watch
- Anker Travel Battery Bank (Its got built in USB-C cables, more on that later)
- iPad Pro (not always, so I keep this as a "stretch goal" item, really depends on the length of flights)
- 1-2 Anker GaN USB-C wall charging blocks (i have a bunch, so its typically a pair, a 65W and a 45W that make it into my bag)

Some share USB-C, one still uses Lightning (looking at you, AirPods Max), and the Apple Watch needs its magnetic puck. Then there's the optimization puzzle: which wall block powers what, what can charge through the battery bank's passthrough, what needs to swap in the morning after something else finishes charging overnight.

I've done this mental exercise dozens of times without ever write it down, so I re-derive it from scratch every trip: Spend 15 minutes staring at cables, grab 3 of each, and inevitably throwing in one extra charger "just in case."

## This Time I Gave the Problem to Claude

I opened Claude and typed out my devices, their charging inputs, and the wall blocks I own. No spreadsheet, no diagram, just a plain description of the constraints:

```prompt title="Claude Desktop Prompt"
Here are my devices and what charges them. Here are the wall blocks I have. The Anker battery bank has 2 built-in USB-C output cables and supports passthrough charging. Minimize what I pack.
```

Claude thought for a bit and gave me a planned out version of the full charging topology: which block powers what, what daisychains through the battery bank, what swaps in the morning after the bigger/more important charges finish overnight.

The answer was **2 wall blocks + 1 battery bank + 3 cables**. Six items to charge seven devices.

## The Charging Tree

The key insight (which I'd vaguely known but never committed to memory fully) is treating the battery bank as a charging _hub_, not just a portable backup. With passthrough enabled:

One wall outlet gives me three devices charging simultaneously (mostly, the battery wont charge itself if the power draw exceeds the power input). The second outlet runs the 45W block dedicated to the Apple Watch via its magnetic cable.

In the morning, the iPad is done. Swap it for the iPhone. The Apple Watch is done, swap the 45W block's cable for a USB-C to Lightning and top off the AirPods Max. By checkout, everything is at 100%.

## From Text to Interactive Diagram

I liked the setup enough that I wanted a visual reference I could glance at instead of puling up a note on my phone. So I asked Claude to build an interactive diagram that walks through each phase: overnight, morning swap, travel, and a packing checklist.

It built a tabbed React component with the full charging topology diagrammed for each scenario. I dropped it onto my site as a standalone tool.

**Check it out: [Charging Setup Diagram](/dev/charging-setup)**

Now when I'm packing, I pull up the diagram, follow the overnight tab to plug everything in, and check the pack list tab before I zip the bag. No re-deriving, no second-guessing.

## Why This Matters (To Me, Anyway)

This isn't a hard problem. It's a _tedious_ problem. The kind of thing that's not worth building a spreadsheet for, but annoying enough that you waste 15 minutes on it every time. The mental overhead isn't the difficulty, it's that I have to re-derive the same answer from scratch because I never bother to formalize it.

Describing the problem to Claude took about two minutes. Getting the optimized setup plus an interactive reference tool took maybe ten. That's a permanent solve for a recurring annoyance, and the activation energy was basically zero.

The best part is that when my device lineup changes (goodbye Lightning AirPods Max, eventually) I can re-describe the constraints and get an updated setup in minutes. The diagram is easy to regenerate too.
